[This post strays from my typical link-sharing approach to Tumblr.] The presentation focused on the company’s culture and, in particular, how the presenter sees LinkedIn embodying principles that he gleened from Jiro Ono, the master sushi chef and focus of the documentary Jiro Dreams of Sushi. Here are the principles:

  1. Become an expert
  2. Never stop learning, practicing your craft, and evolving
  3. Surround yourself with experts and learn from them
  4. Innovate
  5. Have a stand: develop a value system and stick to it in the face of adversity
  6. Remember that it’s about people
One of the main takeaways that applies to all of these was that LinkedIn encourages its employees to pursue what they’re passionate about within the company in order to improve the company’s end-products and user experience. I also learned a few things that I found interesting:
  • No one at LinkedIn stays on the same team for more than 11 months, and (voluntarily) switching teams sooner than that is the norm.
  • LinkedIn shuts down users’ updates to their network when they indicate that they are searching for a job (a nifty bit of user-centric design that I hadn’t known about before).
  • Everyone has quarterly reviews for promotion, and it isn’t unusual to be promoted after six months or so in a position.
  • Engineers can refuse to build something (i.e. they are a central part of the product team): the presenter told us about a time when he took a design to a developer and was told, “No, I’m not going to build that because it’s not cool.” (This one was my favorite.)
All in all, I was impressed with the culture. This is understatement, but it seems like a cool place to work.
